WORLD SCIENCE DAY

World Science Day for Peace and Development, celebrated annually on November 10, shines a light on the role science plays in advancing society and fostering peace worldwide. Launched by UNESCO in 2001, this day underscores the importance of making scientific knowledge accessible to all and ensuring that people can make informed decisions about issues affecting their lives. Through various events and educational programs, World Science Day raises awareness about how science can be harnessed to tackle pressing global challenges, including climate change, health crises, and food security. It also serves as a reminder that scientific progress is essential to sustainable development and improving human well-being


The Purpose of the Day is to:
Strengthen public awarness of the role of science for peaceful and sustainable societies;
Promote national and international solidarity for shared science between countires;
Renew national and international commitment for the use of science for the benefit of societies;
Draw attention to the challenges faced by science in raising support for the scientific endeavour.

Beyond highlighting the importance of science, World Science Day promotes collaboration and inclusivity within the scientific community. By encouraging partnerships between scientists, governments, and communities, it fosters a cooperative approach to addressing the world’s most significant challenges. This day also underscores the need for ethical and socially responsible science practices, which consider the environmental and social impacts of scientific endeavors. Ultimately, World Science Day aims to inspire young people to pursue careers in science and to ensure that science remains a force for positive change in the world, promoting a peaceful and sustainable future for all.

SCIENTISTS

Scientists are the curious minds driving discoveries that shape our understanding of the world and propel innovation across every field. Through rigorous study, experimentation, and analysis, they investigate complex questions about everything from the inner workings of cells to the vastness of the cosmos. Working in laboratories, research facilities, or in the field, scientists rely on critical thinking, patience, and creativity to push the boundaries of what’s known. Their contributions lead to advancements in medicine, technology, environmental protection, and countless other areas that improve human life and help solve global challenges. By communicating their findings and collaborating internationally, scientists not only deepen our knowledge but also inspire future generations to pursue science, ensuring the continued pursuit of truth, progress, and sustainability.

Logie Baird (1888-1946)

John Logie Baird FRSE (/ˈloʊɡi bɛərd/;[1] 13 August 1888 – 14 June 1946) was a Scottish inventor, electrical engineer, and innovator who demonstrated the world's first live working television system on 26 January 1926. He went on to invent the first publicly demonstrated colour television system and the first viable purely electronic colour television picture tube.

Graham Bell (1847-1922)

Alexander Graham Bell (/ˈɡreɪ.əm/, born Alexander Bell; March 3, 1847 – August 2, 1922)[4] was a Scottish-born Canadian-American inventor, scientist, and engineer who is credited with patenting the first practical telephone. He also co-founded the American Telephone and Telegraph Company (AT&T) in 1885.

Joseph Black (1728-1799)

Joseph Black (16 April 1728 – 6 December 1799) was a Scottish physicist and chemist, known for his discoveries of magnesium, latent heat, specific heat, and carbon dioxide. He was Professor of Anatomy and Chemistry at the University of Glasgow for 10 years from 1756, and then Professor of Medicine and Chemistry at the University of Edinburgh from 1766, teaching and lecturing there for more than 30 years.

Alexander Fleming (1881-1955)

Sir Alexander Fleming FRS FRSE FRCS[2] (6 August 1881 – 11 March 1955) was a Scottish physician and microbiologist, best known for discovering the world's first broadly effective antibiotic substance, which he named penicillin. His discovery in 1928 of what was later named benzylpenicillin (or penicillin G) from the mould Penicillium rubens has been described as the "single greatest victory ever achieved over disease". For this discovery, he shared the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1945 with Howard Florey and Ernst Chain.

James Clerk Maxwell (1831-1879)

James Clerk Maxwell FRS FRSE (13 June 1831 – 5 November 1879) was a Scottish physicist and mathematician[1][2] who was responsible for the classical theory of electromagnetic radiation, which was the first theory to describe electricity, magnetism and light as different manifestations of the same phenomenon. Maxwell's equations for electromagnetism have been called the "second great unification in physics" where the first one had been realised by Isaac Newton.
